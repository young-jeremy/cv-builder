Tailor Each Cover Letter

Customize your cover letter for each job application. Research the company and position to highlight relevant skills and experiences that match the job requirements.

Address the Hiring Manager

Whenever possible, address your cover letter to a specific person rather than using "To Whom It May Concern." Research the company website or call to find the hiring manager's name.

Keep It Concise

Limit your cover letter to one page with 3-4 paragraphs. Be clear and concise, focusing on your most relevant qualifications and achievements for the position.

Highlight Achievements

Don't just list job duties. Showcase specific accomplishments with measurable results that demonstrate your value. Use numbers and percentages when possible.

Proofread Carefully

Errors can immediately disqualify you. Proofread multiple times, use spell check, and ask someone else to review your cover letter before submitting.

End With a Call to Action

Conclude with a strong closing paragraph that expresses your interest in an interview and provides your contact information. Be confident but not presumptuous.

Frequently Asked Questions

Your cover letter should be concise and to the point, typically no more than one page. Aim for 3-4 paragraphs that highlight your most relevant qualifications and achievements. Recruiters often review many applications, so keeping your cover letter brief but impactful increases the chances it will be read completely.

Generally, it's best to avoid mentioning salary requirements in your cover letter unless the job posting specifically asks for this information. Discussing compensation too early in the process can limit your negotiating power later. If the job posting requires you to include salary expectations, provide a range rather than a specific figure, and indicate that it's negotiable based on the total compensation package.

Yes, you should customize your cover letter for each job application. While this doesn't mean writing a completely new letter each time, you should tailor the content to address the specific requirements and qualifications mentioned in the job posting. Research the company and position to highlight relevant skills and experiences that match what they're looking for. This targeted approach shows employers that you're genuinely interested in their specific position rather than mass-applying to jobs.

While it's best to address your cover letter to a specific person, sometimes this information isn't available. In these cases, use a professional greeting such as "Dear Hiring Manager," "Dear [Department] Team," or "Dear Hiring Committee." Avoid outdated salutations like "To Whom It May Concern" or "Dear Sir/Madam." Before resorting to a general greeting, try to find the hiring manager's name by checking the company website, LinkedIn, or calling the company directly.

It's generally not necessary to address employment gaps directly in your cover letter unless they are recent or significant. Your cover letter should focus on your qualifications and enthusiasm for the position. If you do decide to address a gap, be brief and positive, focusing on any relevant skills or experiences gained during that time (such as volunteer work, education, or freelance projects). Save detailed explanations for the interview, where you can provide context in a conversation rather than in writing.
